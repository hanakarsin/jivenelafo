What is equimarginal principle in economics?

July 14, 2024
Post

How one allocates his income into buying various goods is also known as the consumer's equilibrium position to the purchase of those goods. At this point, the equimarginal principle comes in play whereby the consumer is assumed to be a rational being capable of substituting one commodity for another to maximize utility.

The principle of equimarginal utility holds a special place in the analysis of negligible utility. Consumer's equilibrium is explained through this principle. It describes how a consumer with a given amount of income spends the money to purchase various goods and services he needs.

What does equimarginal mean?

Equimarginal principle is primarily used in managerial, consumer, and environmental economics. Equimarginal law states that a consumer is obliged to choose the combination of goods that will help in maximizing the total utility of those goods. Here are some crucial factors to consider:

  • Consumers consider the negligible utility of the goods and the prices
  • The consumer, in effect, will be evaluating the negligible utility over the cost of the commodities
  • The result is referred to as the negligible utility of expenditure on each commodity

What is equimarginal principle in economics?

In economics, the law explains how a consumer behaves while distributing his limited income to purchase various goods and services. It is an extension of the law of diminishing marginal utility of assets or commodities.

The negligible utility goes hand in hand with diminishing marginal returns. It means that the consumer is expected to experience diminishing returns in most goods. Therefore, the negligible utility of the sixth good is expected to be lower than the negligible utility of the first good.

Assumptions of marginal utility theory

The theory of marginal utility assumes the following:

  • Consumers of products are rational beings
  • The efficiency is explained in terms of cardinal terms such as monetary units
  • The prices of goods and the income of the consumer are constant
  • The commodities can be divided into smaller units.

The challenge of evaluating utility

When buying goods, consumers don't always have a rough idea of the amount of service they will obtain. Practically, they are not able to calculate the numerical value of the commodity's service.

Lack of time to calculate negligible price or utility

Most of the time, the decision to purchase a particular commodity isn't always informed by calculating the marginal utility but by consumer's habits or guts.

Consumers aren't rational always

The theory of negligible utility assumes that the consumer is an intelligent being, which isn't still the case. At times, the decision of the consumer is influenced by other things such as impulse or advertisement.

Numerous commodities

Away from the assumption world, consumers have inconsistent income while the products to choose from are multiple, making it difficult to undertake even the rough estimations.

Many commodities are related

The utility of specific products is dependent on some variables. For instance, the efficiency of a video recorder depends on the quality of the cassette produced.

Equimarginal principle in environmental economics

In environmental economics, the equimarginal principle aids in the reduction of cost. The total cost that is used to minimize the allocation of reduction amongst various pollutants should be carried out in a way that the managerial abatement cost is equal in all plants.

The principal primarily focuses on the cost-effectiveness in abatement reduction.

Example

A firm has two pollution generating plants, namely A and B. The negligible abatement cost (NAC) is the cost incurred in the reduction of one more unit of wastage, which is pollution in this particular case.

Here is the negligible abatement cost schedule for A and B:

  • NAC of A per unit: 0.50, 0.60, 0.70, 0.80, 0.90
  • NAC of B per unit: 0.60, 0.75, 0.85, 0.90. 1.00
  • Pollution units to be reduced for A and B: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5

Equimarginal principle in managerial economics

The first equimarginal principle concept is widely used in managerial economics, which is also known as the principle of maximum satisfaction. Equimarginal law in managerial economics states that any input allocation should be undertaken in a manner that the value added by the last input unit is similar in all uses. Therefore, a base for maximum exploitation of all inputs is put in place to attain maximum profit.

Equimarginal principle in managerial economics can be used in different aspects of management such as:

  • Budgeting: Resources are allocated in areas in which they are most productive.
  • Elimination of waste: The law aids in eliminating resources that have been allocated in useless and less profitable activities in the firm.
  • Investment: The management accepts investments that produce high returns so that maximum capital allocation is considered in such investments.
  • Multiple products pricing: A multiple-product firm reaches its equilibrium if the negligible revenue of one commodity is equal to the negligible receipt of the other merchandise or commodities.
