Opinion: COVID-19 at one in Kenya, we've been through a dark patch but still, we rise

May 23, 2024
Post

Kenyans, like many other people across the world, ushered in the year 2020 with a lot of hope for growth, not only age-wise but also socially, economically and even spiritually.

COVID-19, a strange yet deadly virus that had caused hundreds of deaths in Wuhan China from around November 2019, barely featured in our minds. But things changed fast and the first cases of the coronavirus were reported in Egypt and Algeria in February 2020, causing a wave of panic across Africa.

As the virus continued to spread across the continent, our worst fears of having the virus within our boundaries were confirmed on Friday, March 13, 2020, when Health Cabinet Secretary Mutahi Kagwe announced that the first Kenyan, a 27-year-old African had contracted the virus. Fear and a dark cloud engulfed all the spaces in our beloved country.

On Sunday, March 15, 2020, two more cases were reported, heightening the fear among Kenyans.

On March 15, 2021, President Uhuru Kenyatta ordered the closure of learning institutions from the following week.

It was the first time a majority of Kenyans, I included, had experienced the blanket closure of schools, places of worship and other institutions by the head of state.

Parents were scared to the core. They were scared of their children’s welfare at school and journey back home and confused about what the future held. The youths in higher learning institutions as well as those employed or jobless stared at uncertain days.

A cloud of uncertainty hung low over every head, bearing in mind that very little was known about this virus.

Even the Western countries perceived to be superior were recording high infection rates and subsequent deaths.

Nobody had a better understanding of the disease that was felling people in Italy, shaking the US and the UK, pricking Russia and whirling in South Africa.

While I reeled under the weight of fear on Sunday, March 15, 2020, I, in hindsight, now feel I was lucky to have been spared the apocalyptic visions some people, including scientists, endured.

Strolling in my Eastlands backyard that evening, shortly after Uhuru announced the first containment protocols orders, I could see faces spelling the word fear in capital letters. The hood is usually full of life.

Some people were gathered in small groups talking in low tones and would intermittently widen the spaces between them as they came to terms with the term social distancing.

The local supermarkets were flooded with locals scrambling for dry foods and handwashing soaps whose prices had shot up.

Cereals’ traders enjoyed booming business but were not excited as they never knew what tomorrow would bring. Sanitisers were missing in mtaani outlets and hand wash liquids and methylated spirits sold like fresh managu from Kisii County.

I remember receiving an email from our CEO directing us to work from home as we continued to assess the situation. Adapting to remote working came with its fair share of challenges, lessons, and good times, in different measure. It was weird right? There were frantic efforts to adapt to what continues to be the new normal one year later.

Relatives upcountry called regularly, asking how the city was; they were scared for us and we acted strong but the fear within was obvious. The city was the epicentre of the ‘foreign’ virus. Mbagathi Hospital became a household, but dreaded name.

We learnt to spell, pronounce and live the words, quarantine, isolation etc. The then newly appointed Kagwe was revered - his speeches penetrated many ears than religious sermons.

His words of hope inspired scared Kenyans more than Obama’s ‘Yes We Can’ slogan did in 2008.

Afya House doors looked like an entrance to a place called hope. Who didn’t have panic attacks and imaginary symptoms of COVID-19? You experienced one symptom and your mind added imaginary ones and somehow you just felt unwell. It was all in the mind.

And who ever thought that flu, which is common in this cold Nairobi, would make your friends and neighbours treat you like a leper?

Then the sad and dreaded began to happen. People doubted the existence of the disease asking each other if they knew anyone who had contracted the virus. The government's shaky reputation was gas to this flame of doubt and as this continued as the virus attacked people we know.

I never doubted the disease because by week two, a person I knew got infected and spent well over 21 days in a High Dependency Unit (HDU) and Intensive Care Unit (ICU) respectively.

Lockdowns came and the economy fell apart like Okonkwo’s perfect but marinated aping of his father Unoka’s failure.

And the Big fish were at it again: they swindled, robbed and chewed grants meant to alleviate the negative economic effects of the virus on the vulnerable groups in society.

But with a veil of ignorance, the virus equalised and dealt with the rich and the poor in equal measure as days went by.

Sadly, this was the only time our country got close to living the equality dream; albeit in a wicked way.

Hospitals were terrible places to be in. They became isolation and quarantine places.

Churches and places of worship were restricted zones and with this, hope became difficult to find. Visionary and financially stable churches shifted online and continued preaching the gospel and emphasised the true religion of helping widows and orphans as written in the biblical book of James.

With curfew declarations, the security personnel did not hesitate in doing what they had to do to maintain law and order.

The news was heartbreaking: limited beds, overwhelmed medics and deaths became part of the story shortly after Brian's and Brenda Cherotich's televised recovery had brought so much hope.

Tanzanians put hope in God and prayed like Kenyans and Ugandans did but disregarded the fact that doctors were also created and gifted by God and have a role to play in treating ailments.

A year later, some of us who had praised our faith and castigated Uhuru for enforcing restrictive measures have a different story to tell.

It was a year of the bad getting worse. Curfews and restricted movement meant the closure of businesses, loss of jobs, salary cuts, inability to pay rent, buy food and sleep peacefully.

Urban dwellers witnessed nightmares during the day and stayed awake through the night. We buried friends and family and postponed weddings and pre-wedding ceremonies. Some families disintegrated as domestic violence and divorce cases reigned supreme.

Depression levels rose radically. Christmas wasn't just what we had back in the days - there was little to celebrate just as there was little money in people’s pockets.

Dreams got shattered in 2020 and everyone's prayer was for the year to end, with the hope that it would fly away with corona and usher in new beginnings of revival.

Today, Saturday, March 13 is exactly one year since the first case of COVID-19 was reported and trends like the curfew and putting on masks have been normalized.

The disease is still here with us but the fear has faded away, masks prices have nosedived like our politicians’ morals during the pandemic.

On Saturday, March 13, 870 people tested positive for the disease, bringing the total confirmed positive cases in the country to 112,805 with 1,364,699 cumulative tests.

Seven patients succumbed on Saturday, pushing the cumulative fatalities in the country to 1,908.

But what gives hope, way beyond the vaccine that arrived in early March, is the high recovery rate, with the country having recorded 88,326 recoveries in 365 days.

Businesses are responding to resuscitation measures, job adverts are returning, schools are back on course and our minds yearn for a return to the former normal. The journey continues.

The future belongs to those who are determined to relaunch their dreams but at a different level, not the pre-COVID-19 levels.

For many people, the year was lost and relationships broken, but God is in the business of restoration.

It's just about us living the word. If you've seen March 2021, you're blessed and God still has some good plans for you.

And as we hope and continue to adhere to new restrictions issued by Uhuru on Friday, March 12, let’s put God first, way above the political leadership and everything else.

To those who are sick, we pray for your healing, and to those bereaved, be comforted; things will get better.

Fellow Kenyans, let’s hold hands as we steadily rise from the ruins and ashes. Do not forget to wear your mask, sanitize and observe social distance.
